Internship

During my second year of CPES, I completed a UROP internship in cognitive sciences under Olivier Morin, Research Director at CNRS at the Jean Nicod Institute. Titled "Beauty and Combinatoriality," this internship aimed to study the perception of beauty, particularly in repetitive patterns such as friezes, like the one shown on the right.

I developed a rigorous method for annotating decorative friezes, allowing them to be classified according to their symmetries1 and quantitatively compared across different cultures and periods. I applied this method to several visual corpora, including Le Dictionnaire de l'Ornement by Albert Racinet, as well as a database on Roman mosaics2, annotating several hundred friezes to extract several thousand motifs. These data will subsequently be used to test different theoretical models on human aesthetic preferences and their cognitive foundations.

This internship allowed me to gain experience in the world of research and to learn how to apply the experimental method, notably through reading scientific articles and formulating hypotheses. I developed my critical thinking and deepened my knowledge in art history, mathematics, and cognitive sciences. I also acquired practical skills in using software tools and in annotating data, both manually and automatically. Additionally, I discovered document research, particularly at the library of the Quai Branly Museum. These experiences provided me with a more comprehensive understanding of research work and strengthened my ability to analyze and organize complex data, as well as my autonomy.

1. Washburn, D. K., & Crowe, D. W. (1988). Symmetries of culture: Theory and practice of plane pattern analysis. University of Washington Press.

2. Balmelle, Catherine, and Richard Prudhomme. Le décor géométrique de la mosaïque romaine. Vol. 1. Répertoire graphique et descriptif des compositions linéaires et isotropes.
